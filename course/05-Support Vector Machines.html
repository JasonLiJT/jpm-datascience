Support Vector Machines

About this module

Support Vector Machines (SVMs) are a powerful supervised learning technique commonly used for classification and regression. Initially introduced for binary classification (Vapnik and Chervonenkis, 1963), SVMs became increasingly popular in the scientific community in the 1990s and have demonstrated empirically good performance in a wide range of fields such as image recognition, text classification and bioinformatics, among others.

In this module you will focus on using SVMs for classification, learn how to implement linear and non-linear boundaries, explore different "off-the-shelf" kernels and tune their hyperparameters, and investigate multi-class problems.

Margin maximisation and hyperplanes

The intuition

Let us first assume an ideal hypothetical scenario, where the given training data points of different classes in a binary classification problem are perfectly linearly separable as depicted in Figure 1. As with other classifiers, during the modelling process the SVM classifier attempts to construct a boundary that separates the instances of different classes as accurately as possible (minimising misclassifications). In a two-dimensional space and for a linearly separable case such as the one of Figure 1, the separating boundary is a straight line. In higher dimensions, the boundary is a hyperplane (generalisation of a straight line).

Figure 1. A synthetic linearly separable binary classification problem

In this case, there are multiple possible linear separators that can accurately separate the instances of the two classes as you can see from Figure 2. Is any of them "better" than the others? How could we determine an optimal hyperplane?

Intuitively, a decision boundary drawn in the middle of the distance between the two distinct classes (line "3") appears to be better than one which approaches too close to the instances of the two classes (e.g. line "1").

Figure 2. A subset of linear decision boundaries that can accurately separate the instances of the two classes. Is there an optimal one?

The core concept behind the success and the powerful nature of Support Vector Machines is that of margin maximisation. More specifically, SVMs attempt to build a decision boundary that accurately separates the training samples of different classes by maximising the margin between them. The margin is the (perpendicular) distance from the decision surface to the closest data points of each class.

Figure 3. SVM boundaries attempt to find the optimal separating hyperplane that accurately separates the training samples of different classes by maximising the margin between the support vectors.
Note

It is important to note that the decision boundary of a SVM classifier is entirely determined by a (usually fairly small) subset of the training instances - known as the support vectors.

If a support vector is removed from the data set, the boundary is likely to change whereas if any other point is removed, the boundary will not change. This contrasts with many other classification techniques where the removal of any sample would potentially modify the boundary.

Linear SVMs (hard-margin): separable data

Let us assume again the previous hypothetical binary classification problem: the SVM model is given as input a training dataset S=((x1,y1),...,(xn,yn))S=((x1,y1),...,(xn,yn)), where xi∈ℝpxi∈Rp is the set of n input (training) instances and yi∈{−1,+1}yi∈{−1,+1} are their associated labels. The main goal of any SVM model is to find a classification function f(x)f(x) that best fits the dataset (and generalises well!) while maximising the distance (margin) between the nearest points of the two classes such that:

The equation of a hyperplane is:

⟨x,w⟩+b=0,⟨x,w⟩+b=0,

where ⟨x,w⟩=∑pi=1xiwi⟨x,w⟩=∑i=1pxiwi is the inner product of a point xx with the normal vector ww which characterises the slope of the hyperplane and bb is the intercept. Note that the inner product is also sometimes written with a dot: ⟨x,w⟩=x⋅w⟨x,w⟩=x⋅w.

Geometrically, the SVM margin can be shown to be proportional to 1/∥w∥1/∥w∥. Therefore, to maximise the distance between the planes we want to minimise the norm (length) ∥w∥∥w∥. This is a constrained optimisation problem which can be written

min12∥w∥2min12∥w∥2

subject to yi(⟨w,xi⟩+b)≥1,i=1,…,n.subject to yi(⟨w,xi⟩+b)≥1,i=1,…,n.

This problem involves the optimisation of a quadratic objective function subject to linear inequality constraints. Quadratic optimisation problems are a well-known class of mathematical programming problems for which there exists well established solvers (such as IBM’s CPLEX or MOSEK).

Linear SVMs (soft-margin): non-separable data

The previous section provided an overview of linear SVM classifiers when applied to separable data (i.e., data such that there exists at least one hyperplane separating the two classes exactly). However, real-life scenarios tend to be complex and separation between different classes is not so straightforward.

In order to address cases of non-separable data while still using a linear model, the implementation of SVMs can be expanded with the addition of slack variables ξiξi. The slack variables relax the hard-margin constraints where misclassifications are not allowed, leading to softer margins that tolerate misclassifications to a particular degree. A regularisation parameter C>0C>0, also known as the cost or penalty error, determines the trade-off between margin maximisation and training error toleration. If CC is sufficiently large, the soft-margin SVM will put the emphasis on minimising the number of misclassifications at the expense of the margin. By contrast, if CC is close to zero, the emphasis will be on maximising the margins while being more tolerant to misclassifications.

Figure 5. Soft-margin SVM that allows some misclassifications (at some cost defined by the parameter CC).

Soft-margin SVMs can be formulated as follow:

min12∥w∥2+C∑i=1Nξimin12∥w∥2+C∑i=1Nξi

subject to yi(w⋅xi+b)≥1−ξi,ξi≥0,i=1,…,nsubject to yi(w⋅xi+b)≥1−ξi,ξi≥0,i=1,…,n

which, again, can be tackled using standard numerical optimisation libraries.

Non-linear (kernel) SVM

Let us assume a hypothetical case of a non-linearly separable case study such as the one of Figure 7. The input space in this case cannot be separated well by a linear classifier and yet we can obviously see a clear separation between the two classes.

Figure 7. A hypothetical non-separable binary classification problem where linear separation is not ideal.

This can be done with SVMs by exploiting the kernel trick. Instead of attempting to construct a boundary in the initial input space, the data are mapped from the input space X into a transformed feature space H, where linear separation is potentially feasible. This can be done using a non-linear function ϕ:→ϕ:X→H as represented in Figure 8. The back-projection of the optimal separating hyperplane from the new feature space to the original input space generates a non-linear boundary of given complexity.

Figure 8. The kernel trick transforms the data in a feature space where the instances from the two classes may be linearly separable. These examples are based on the tutorial "Everything You Wanted to Know about the Kernel Trick".

In order to perform classification using a SVM in the feature space, it is only necessary to know the kernel function which is related to the non-linear feature mapping as follows:

K(xi,xj)=⟨ϕ(xi),ϕ(xj)⟩.K(xi,xj)=⟨ϕ(xi),ϕ(xj)⟩.

There is a wide range of widely-used predefined ("off-the-shelf") kernel functions corresponding to a range of non-linear mapping. The most commonly applied kernels include:

  • Gaussian Radial Basis Function (RBF): K(xi,xj)=exp(−γ∥xi−xj∥2)K(xi,xj)=exp⁡(−γ∥xi−xj∥2)

  • Polynomial: K(xi,xj)=(γxTixj+c)dK(xi,xj)=(γxiTxj+c)d

  • Sigmoid: K(xi,xj)=tanh(γxTixj+c)K(xi,xj)=tanh⁡(γxiTxj+c)

Obviously determining the appropriate kernel for the problem at hand is difficult in general and several kernels may be tried until an appropriate separation accuracy is obtained.

Figure 9. "Off-the-shelf" SVM kernels and example of resulting boundaries.

RBF kernel hyperparameters

Every kernel is characterised by a set of hyperparameters that have to be tuned for a particular problem. The Gaussian Radial Basis Function (RBF) kernel is particularly popular especially in cases where there is little or no prior knowledge about the data under study. In RBF SVMs, only one kernel parameter has to be optimised - the value of γγ - in addition to the regularisation parameter CC.

The hyperparameter γγ determines the degree of nonlinearity or width of the RBF kernel.

  • Higher values of γγ lead to greater nonlinearity, sharper peaks and "spiky" boundaries that surround individual instances.

  • Lower values of γγ lead to smoother surfaces since the RBF kernel tends towards a linear boundary.

The cost or regularisation parameter CC controls the trade-off between maximising the margin and minimising the training error.

  • As CC increases, tolerance of misclassifications is lower and usually the hard-margin case is obtained. The high values of CC will force the creation of complex boundaries that misclassify as few training samples as possible (possibly leading to cases of overfitting).

  • As CC decreases, wider margins are created, but if it is too small, there may be underfitting.

Figure 10. The effect of tuning the hyperparameters on the SVM boundaries. Figure extracted from http://scikit-learn.org/stable/_images/sphx_glr_plot_rbf_parameters_001.png

Non-linear (RBF) SVMs in scikit-learn

In addition to the regularisation parameter C, which is common to all types of SVM, the gamma hyperparameter in the RBF kernel controls the nonlinearity of the SVM boundaries. The larger the gamma, the more non-linear the boundaries surrounding individual samples. Lower values of gamma lead to broader, more linear boundaries.

First let us build an RBF SVM model (set the kernel parameter to rbf) using the default values for the hypeparameters C (C=1.0) and gamma (gamma='auto'). Comprehensive documentation on how to implement SVMs with scikit-learn can be found here.

Hyperparameter tuning for non-linear SVMs

In scikit-learn hyperparameters are passed as arguments to the constructor of the estimator classes. The full list of SVM parameters available in scikit-learn can be found on the corresponding SkLearn documentation page.

For the RBF kernel, proper selection of C and gamma is critical and can greatly affect the performance of the classification model. These values are not known a priori and are highly data-dependent. Optimisation (tuning) of the hyperparameters can be achieved by applying most commonly a coarse tuning in the form of a grid search (often followed by a finer-tuning in the "neighborhood" of good parameters) using the GridSearchCV function we applied earlier. Very good documentation on the tuning of the SVM hyperparameters in scikit-learn can be found here.

As described in the paper by Hsu et al., 2003, trying exponentially growing sequences of CC and γγ is a practical method to yield good parameters; so, the parameters you will feed into GridSearchCV will take the following ranges of values C∈[2−5,2−3,...215C∈[2−5,2−3,...215 ] and γ∈[2−15,2−3,...25γ∈[2−15,2−3,...25 ].

This may take a few minutes to run.

# Define the parameters to be optimised and their values/ranges
# Range for gamma and Cost hyperparameters

g_range = 2. ** np.arange(-15, 5, step=2)
C_range = 2. ** np.arange(-5, 15, step=2)
parameters = [{'gamma': g_range, 'C': C_range}]

grid = GridSearchCV(SVC(), parameters, cv=5, n_jobs=4)
grid.fit(XTrain, yTrain)

bestG = grid.best_params_['gamma']
bestC = grid.best_params_['C']
print "The best parameters are: gamma=", np.log2(bestG), " and Cost=", np.log2(bestC)

Multi-class SVMs

SVMs are inherently binary classifiers, where the class labels can only take two values {−1,+1}{−1,+1}. Most real-world problems often require discrimination of more than two categories so over the years the SVM functionality was extended to allow multi-class cases. Most multi-class SVM algorithms decompose the multi-class problem into a series of binary problems that standard SVM implementation can be applied to. The most popular and widely-used techniques for multi-class problems are the "one-against-all" and "one-against-one".

The "one-against-all" (or "one-versus-all" or "one-versus-rest") approach was the earliest and simplest method proposed, which involves determining how well a sample is modelled by each class individually, and subsequently selecting the class it is modelled-by at its best. in this case, for a NN-class problem, NN binary classifiers are created and trained, one for each given class.

The "one-against-one" (or "one-versus-one") approach constructs several binary SVM classifiers for each available pairwise combination of classes and the results of these individual classifiers are aggregated using a voting mechanism such as the "majority vote". In this case, N(N−1)/2N(N−1)/2 SVM models are created, one for each pairwise combination of classes.

In scikit-learn, SVC and NuSVC implement and use the "one-against-one" approach for multi-class classification. On the other hand, LinearSVC implements the "one-against-all" multi-class strategy; the LinearSVC also implements an alternative multi-class strategy, the so-called multi-class SVM formulated by Crammer and Singer, by using the option multi_class='crammer_singer'. More information on how multi-class classification is performed in scikit-learn can be found at http://scikit-learn.org/stable/modules/svm.html.

Wrap up of Module 5

  • SVMs attempt to build a decision boundary that accurately separates the training samples of different classes by maximising the margin between them.

  • Margins can be “hard” or “soft”. Slack variables "relax" the hard-margin constraints where misclassifications are not allowed, leading to softer margins that tolerate misclassifications to a degree.

  • The regularisation parameter C>0C>0 determines the trade-off between margin maximisation and training error tolerance. A low CC tolerates training misclassifications and allows softer margins, while for high CC the misclassifications become more significant leading to hard-margin SVMs and potentially cases of overfitting.

  • The kernel trick is helpful to transform the data in a feature space where the instances from the two classes may be better linearly separated. Examples of popular Kernel functions include Gaussian Radial Basis Function (RBF), Polynomial, and Sigmoid.

  • Most multi-class SVM algorithms decompose the multi-class problem into a series of binary problems that standard SVM implementation can be applied to.
